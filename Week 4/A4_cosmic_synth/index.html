Pick a calendar day. We pull open space data for that day and turn it into a short loop you can hear and watch.

What is this? How does it work?

Cosmic Synth is a small browser toy: you choose a date, it fetches public space listings for that day, and plays a four-layer loop while a simple orbit drawing reacts. No astronomy background required.

Asteroids (near-Earth) are small bodies—mostly rock or metal—whose orbits can bring them close to Earth. NASA publishes daily lists; we use them for drums (not a collision forecast). Pulsars are ultra-dense star leftovers that spin hundreds of times per second and sweep a beam of radio energy past us like a lighthouse; we borrow a real catalog pulsar to set beats per minute. Moon phase is how lit the Moon looks from Earth. NASA’s DONKI feed lists solar flares and geomagnetic storms by date; we score that day lightly (big flares, high Kp index) and map it to the same calm / wide / intense pad moods as before.

Use Generate to load data, then Play. Hover any readout row or track name for plain-English help. The same date usually gives the same loop until the live data changes.

Type the date as digits; slashes fill in for you.

We highly recommend a free personal NASA API key when you have a minute—optional, but nice if you keep trying new dates.
